# Level 1: Foundational Philosophy
<!-- L1: WHY & WHAT EXPERIENCE — External references only -->

## Competitors

- **ADRs (Architecture Decision Records)** — Lightweight markdown records of decisions; no hierarchy, no validation, no living workflow
- **Notion/Confluence specs** — Document-based specs that drift from code; no enforcement mechanism
- **GitHub Issues + PRs** — Tracks work but not intent; decisions and reasoning scatter across threads
- **PlantUML / Mermaid diagrams** — Visualizes structure but doesn't validate philosophy-to-implementation alignment
- **Shape Up (Basecamp)** — Structured product thinking but no machine-readable output or validation

## Table Stakes

- A way to define what a product is and why it exists
- Structured hierarchy from philosophy to implementation
- Testable acceptance criteria tied to the spec
- Works with AI coding tools (Copilot, Claude, Cursor)
- No proprietary lock-in — plain text, version-controlled

## Pain Points

- **Spec drift** — Written specs diverge from shipped code within weeks; nobody reads the Confluence page
- **Philosophy-implementation gap** — Implementation decisions contradict product philosophy because nobody checked
- **Unreliable AI output** — AI generates code confidently but has no awareness of product intent or constraints
- **Untraceable decisions** — A feature ships but nobody can explain why it works the way it does
- **Gatekeeper fatigue** — Process tools that block work rather than guide it breed resentment and workarounds

## Influences

- **Dependency Inversion Principle** — High-level policy should not depend on low-level details
- **Executable specifications (BDD/Gherkin)** — The spec is both human-readable and machine-runnable
- **RFC process** — Structured, versioned, philosophy-first decision-making
- **Literate programming** — Documentation and code as a unified artifact

---

## Mission

RootSpec exists to close the gap between human intent and machine execution — giving developers and teams a structured way to define what they're building and why, so that AI tools, tests, and implementations can all be validated against a single source of truth.

---

## Design Pillars

### 1. Clarity Without Ceremony
Users feel instantly oriented — the methodology is legible on first contact without reading documentation.

**User perspective:** "I understand what this is and how it works before I finish reading the page."

**Examples:**
- Visual hierarchy mirrors the spec hierarchy
- Interactive demos show the methodology in action, not just describe it
- Language is direct and technical without being academic

### 2. Proof Over Promise
Users feel the methodology is credible because the site itself demonstrates it, not just claims it.

**User perspective:** "This site is the argument. If RootSpec built this, it can build what I need."

**Examples:**
- Meta banner makes the demo nature explicit and honest
- Rough edges are acknowledged as part of the demo, not hidden
- Links to actual spec files and seed let visitors audit the claim

### 3. Empowered Curiosity
Users feel invited to explore and experiment, not lectured at.

**User perspective:** "I want to try this on my own project. I know how to start."

**Examples:**
- Interactive hierarchy explorer makes the abstraction tangible
- Spec wizard lets users apply the methodology to their own idea
- CTA is clear: start with one command, not a sales call

### 4. Earned Trust
Users feel the methodology has been stress-tested, not invented yesterday.

**User perspective:** "This was thought through by someone who has been burned by the problems it solves."

**Examples:**
- Problem section names real, specific pain — not vague industry speak
- Tone is a practitioner explaining to peers, not a product marketing page
- Version badge and spec files visible — the methodology is versioned and auditable

---

## Inviolable Principles

- Never hide rough edges — the demo is honest about what minimal guidance produces
- Never use buzzwords or corporate marketing language — speak as a developer to developers
- Never promise features that require AI or external services — everything runs client-side
- The site itself must be a live demonstration of RootSpec — not just a description of it
- Philosophy must remain stable even as implementation details evolve

---

## North-Star Experience

Six months from now, a developer lands on this site after being frustrated by yet another spec that nobody reads. Within three minutes, they understand what RootSpec is, see a working demonstration of the hierarchy, apply it to their own idea using the wizard, and leave with a concrete next step. They don't need to be sold — they recognize the problem, see the solution, and start.
