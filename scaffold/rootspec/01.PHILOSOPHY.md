# Level 1: Foundational Philosophy

**Product:** RootFeed
**Tagline:** A RootSpec scaffold demo
**Version:** 7.2.5

---

## The Problem

Developers evaluating specification methodologies need concrete evidence that specs can tame existing codebases — not just greenfield projects. The scaffold scenario answers the real question: "I already have routes, data loading, and a framework. What does a spec actually add?"

RootFeed demonstrates that RootSpec can take a working skeleton (five empty routes, some JSON files) and elevate it into a complete, coherent, tested experience through structured specification alone. It is not a real social network. It is proof-of-concept made interactive.

## Who Has This Problem

Developers and tech leads who've inherited or started a project without structured specification — people who wonder whether retrofitting a spec onto existing code is worth the overhead. RootFeed shows them, live, that the answer is yes.

## Design Pillars

### Playful Authenticity
The app feels alive and real despite being a demo — conversations, handles, and threads behave like a genuine feed, not a toy.

### Radical Transparency
Every surface makes the demo framing obvious — there is no ambiguity about what this is or how it was built.

### Effortless Exploration
Moving between posts, profiles, tags, and threads feels natural and fast — the feed invites browsing without friction.

### Confident Simplicity
The experience is lean: no sign-in gates, no backend calls, no complexity theater — what you see is exactly what it is.

## What We Will Never Compromise

- The product must never pretend to be a real social network. Every page carries the demo framing.
- All interactive features (like, bookmark, follow, compose) must work client-side without persistence across page loads — this transparency about ephemerality is a feature, not a bug.
- Attribution to RootSpec must be visible on every page. This is a demo of RootSpec, and that must be obvious.
- The spec must be publicly accessible via the meta banner. The visitor should be able to inspect the spec that drove the implementation.

## Comparable Products (and What They Get Wrong)

- **Bluesky / AT Protocol**: Technically impressive but opaque about how it was built. No demo framing.
- **Twitter (early)**: The emotional simplicity we're inspired by — but built without explicit philosophy documentation.
- **Generic SvelteKit demos**: Usually todo apps or counters. They don't demonstrate specification methodology or real social dynamics.

## Table Stakes

For a developer to consider RootFeed a credible scaffold demo, it must have:
- A working home feed with real-looking posts and authors
- Navigable profiles, post threads, search, and an explore surface
- Interactive engagement (like, bookmark, follow, compose)
- A persistent meta banner explaining the scaffold story
- Public links to the seed, spec, and scaffold commit

## North Star

Six months from now, a developer lands on RootFeed while evaluating RootSpec. They can browse a feed, click into a thread, follow a user, compose a post — and throughout, they see a persistent banner explaining this started as a bare scaffold. They click "View the spec" and see the rootspec/ directory. They think: "I could do this for my existing codebase." That's the entire job of this product.
